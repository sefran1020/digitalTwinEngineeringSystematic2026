# Related Reviews

Several reviews already touch this field, which makes it important to state
precisely what they do and do not establish. Table 1 compares the most relevant
prior syntheses—those addressing digital twins (DTs) in education and the longer
line of comparative laboratory reviews from which the DT question descends—along
the dimension that motivates the present work: whether each weighs the
*methodological quality* of the learning evidence it aggregates, rather than only
cataloguing or counting studies.

**Table 1.** Prior reviews of digital twins and of technology-mediated laboratories in higher education, and whether each appraised the methodological quality of the underlying learning evidence. HE = higher education; SET = science, engineering and technology; DT = digital twin.

| Review | Type | Focus and scope | Appraises evidence quality? | Stated gap |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| [@lampropoulos_virtual_2025] | Scoping | VR in engineering education (342 studies) | No | No methodological-quality analysis of included studies |
| [@tokhayeva_virtualisation_2026] | Systematic | Virtualisation in HE; tools and cost | Partial | Weak primary designs; causal and economic inference limited |
| [@jiang_state_2026] | Bibliometric | VR in HE (1,100 papers) | No | Little empirical work on educational effects |
| [@kumar_karanam_systematic_2025] | Systematic | DT-coupled virtual environments for manufacturing education | No | No cost or scalability appraisal |
| [@chamorro-atalaya_use_2024] | Bibliometric | DT in university teaching and learning (92 papers) | No | No analysis of governance or teacher competence |
| [@omrany_digital_2026] | Systematic | DT for construction education and training (19 studies) | No | Fragmented, largely conceptual literature |
| [@vergara_decade_2025] | Bibliometric | DT in materials science and engineering (1,106 papers) | No | Output mapped; educational outcomes not appraised |
| [@maladzhi_digital_2025] | Systematic (PRISMA) | Digital technologies for practical SET teaching (28 studies) | Yes | Digital tools do not fully replace physical laboratories |
| [@ma_handson_2006] | Comparative review | Hands-on vs simulated vs remote laboratories | No | Establishes taxonomy and comparison; no quality weighting |
| [@brinson_learning_2015] | Review of empirical research | Learning outcomes: non-traditional vs hands-on labs | Partial | Prevailing "no significant difference", but weak designs dominate |
| [@heradio_virtual_2016] | Bibliometric | Virtual and remote labs | No | Trends mapped; primary-study quality not appraised |
| [@potkonjak_virtual_2016] | Review | Virtual laboratories for science, technology and engineering | No | Effectiveness reviewed without formal risk-of-bias assessment |

Two patterns are visible. First, the reviews that *do* weigh evidence quality
(e.g., the PRISMA review of digital practical-teaching technologies, and the
nursing and mobile-VR meta-analyses that apply GRADE-style appraisal
[@maladzhi_digital_2025; @li_effects_2026; @widowati_meta-analysis_2025]) sit
*outside* the digital-twin literature. Second, every review *specific to digital
twins in education*—whether bibliometric [@chamorro-atalaya_use_2024;
@vergara_decade_2025] or systematic [@kumar_karanam_systematic_2025;
@omrany_digital_2026]—characterises or counts the literature without appraising the
rigour of the learning evidence it reports. To our knowledge, then, the present
review is the first synthesis of digital twins in engineering and STEM higher
education to make the *quality* of the learning evidence its organising axis, and to
read architecture, pedagogy and evidence jointly rather than as separate
inventories—directly extending, for digital twins, the evidence-quality concern
that comparative laboratory reviews have raised since Ma & Nickerson and Brinson
[@ma_handson_2006; @brinson_learning_2015; @heradio_virtual_2016].
