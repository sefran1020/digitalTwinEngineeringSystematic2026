\title{Digital Twins in Engineering and STEM Higher Education: A Systematic Review of Architectures, Pedagogy, and the Quality of Learning Evidence}

\begin{abstract}
Practical laboratory training is central to engineering education yet constrained by cost, safety, access, and post-pandemic continuity, and digital twins (DTs)---virtual replicas bidirectionally coupled to physical assets---are increasingly proposed to address these limits. This article systematically reviews how DTs are designed, implemented, and evaluated in higher-education engineering and STEM settings, across four objectives: technological architecture and deployment environments; pedagogy and Industry 4.0/5.0 competencies; empirical learning outcomes; and institutional benefits and barriers. Following PRISMA 2020, five databases were searched; a terminological screen, three-agent large-language-model semantic screening with a moderator (inter-agent Fleiss' $\kappa = 0.60$), and human review yielded 126 eligible records, of which 65 retrievable studies (2018--2026) formed the corpus. The methodological quality of empirical studies was scored on a transparent 0--6 composite and sensitivity-tested, and the synthesis was read through an instructional-alignment lens (constructive alignment, ICAP, and Kirkpatrick's levels). The corpus demonstrates feasibility, engagement, and disciplinary breadth, with a recent shift toward bidirectional, occasionally AI-augmented twins; however, the technical maturity of the twin is decoupled from the rigour of its evaluation---architectures are described more than instrumented, pedagogy is active but seldom named, and the learning evidence is weak-dominated, with only a minority of rigorous studies reporting substantial effects. We map where transfer-oriented, well-reported evidence is most needed and propose a reporting checklist for educational digital twins.
\end{abstract}
