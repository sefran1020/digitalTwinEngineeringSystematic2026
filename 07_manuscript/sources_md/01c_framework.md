# Conceptual Framework

The objection that motivates this section is methodological as much as
substantive. A catalogue of digital-twin (DT) implementations, however complete,
does not by itself tell an engineering educator whether these systems teach, or
how. To read architecture, pedagogy and evidence as parts of a single
instructional system rather than as separate technical and educational
narratives, we adopt three established lenses from the learning sciences and use
them consistently to interpret the synthesis. They are deliberately modest in
number and chosen because each maps onto one face of the review's question.

**Constructive alignment** [@biggs_enhancing_1996; @biggs_teaching_2011] supplies
the organising principle. In Biggs' formulation, a course functions as a system
in which intended learning outcomes, the teaching and learning activities, and
the assessment tasks are mutually aligned: students learn what the activities and
assessment actually require of them, not what the syllabus declares. A DT is, in
these terms, a learning *activity*—often an elaborate one. The pertinent question
is therefore not how sophisticated the twin is, but whether the intended
competencies and the assessment have been aligned with it. This reframing turns
the review's central tension—technical maturity advancing faster than evaluative
rigour—into a question about systemic alignment rather than about technology, and
it connects the corpus to a long-standing concern in engineering education with
the explicit, assessable purposes of laboratory work [@feisel_role_2005].

**The ICAP framework** [@chi_icap_2014] refines how we read the pedagogy of
Objective O2. Decades of evidence establish that active engagement outperforms
passive reception in engineering and STEM instruction [@prince_does_2004;
@freeman_active_2014], yet "active learning" is used so broadly that it obscures
more than it reveals. ICAP distinguishes four modes of cognitive engagement of
rising expected benefit—passive, active, constructive and interactive—on the
basis of what learners observably do with material. Applied to the corpus, it
lets us ask whether DT activities labelled "active" engage students
*constructively* (generating new artefacts or explanations) and *interactively*
(co-constructing through dialogue), or merely keep them behaviourally busy. The
single most capable engagement mode in ICAP—learners constructing the model
itself—is precisely the activity our corpus documents only once, a gap the
framework makes legible.

**Kirkpatrick's levels of evaluation** [@kirkpatrick_kirkpatricks_2016] structure
the appraisal of learning outcomes in Objective O3. The four levels—reaction,
learning, behaviour and results—order evidence by how consequential and how
difficult to obtain it is. Reactions (satisfaction, perceived usefulness) are
the easiest to collect and the least probative; demonstrated behaviour change and
downstream results are the most demanding. This ordering provides a vocabulary
for the review's finding that affective and reaction-level outcomes dominate the
corpus while transfer and retention—the levels that would substantiate the
field's competence claims—are rarely measured.

Together these lenses let the review pose, for educational DTs, the comparative
question that has organised research on non-traditional laboratories for two
decades: whether technology-mediated practical work achieves the learning
outcomes of hands-on work, and on what quality of evidence [@ma_handson_2006;
@brinson_learning_2015]. Accordingly, the four objectives are read as alignment
questions. O1 asks what instructional affordances the architecture actually
provides; O2 asks whether the pedagogy engages students constructively and aligns
competencies to the twin; O3 asks at what Kirkpatrick level, and with what rigour,
learning has been evidenced; and O4 asks whether institutional claims are aligned
with demonstrated outcomes or run ahead of them.

A note on the status of this framework is essential to its honest use. The corpus
was coded inductively: the eight themes (T1–T8) that structure the extraction
were derived from the studies themselves, not deduced from constructive
alignment, ICAP or Kirkpatrick. These three lenses were adopted at the
*synthesis* stage, to interpret the extracted data and to connect the findings to
engineering-education research; they were not imposed on the protocol or used as
an a priori coding scheme. The framework therefore guides interpretation and
discussion, and the mapping of pedagogical labels to ICAP modes and of outcomes
to Kirkpatrick levels is offered as a reading of the evidence, not as a
measurement claimed to be present in the primary studies.
