# Title (recommended)

**Digital Twins in Engineering and STEM Higher Education: A Systematic Review of
Architectures, Pedagogy, and the Quality of Learning Evidence**

*Alternative titles:*
- Educational Digital Twins in Engineering Higher Education: A Systematic Review
 of Techno-Pedagogical Maturity and Empirical Evidence
- Digital Twins in Higher-Education Engineering and STEM: A Systematic Review of
 Promise, Pedagogy and Evidence

# Abstract

Practical laboratory training is central to engineering education yet constrained
by cost, safety, access and post-pandemic continuity. Digital twins (DTs)—virtual
replicas bidirectionally coupled to physical assets—are increasingly proposed to
address these limits, but the educational evidence is dispersed and uneven. This
systematic review characterises how DTs are designed, implemented and evaluated in
higher-education engineering and STEM settings, across four objectives:
technological architecture and deployment environments (O1); pedagogy and Industry
4.0/5.0 competencies (O2); empirical learning outcomes (O3); and institutional
benefits and barriers (O4). Following PRISMA 2020, five databases were searched; a
terminological screen, three-agent semantic screening and human review yielded 126
eligible records, of which 65 retrievable studies (2018–2026) formed the corpus.
Extraction was theme-conditional with double verification; the methodological
quality of empirical studies was scored on a 0–6 composite and sensitivity-tested,
and synthesis combined descriptive profiling, aggregation by objective, and an
exploratory relational analysis (attribute-association network, multiple
correspondence analysis and a capability-by-evidence gap map). The corpus consistently demonstrates feasibility
and engagement and is disciplinarily broad, with a recent shift toward
bidirectional, occasionally AI-augmented twins. However, the technical maturity of
the twin is largely decoupled from the rigour of its evaluation: architectures are
described more than instrumented, pedagogy is active but seldom named, and the
learning evidence is weak-dominated, with only a minority of rigorous studies
reporting substantial effects. Benefit and competence claims outpace demonstrated
outcomes. We conclude that educational DTs are promising but not yet evidentially
consolidated, and we outline priorities for pre-registered, transfer-oriented and
well-reported research.
