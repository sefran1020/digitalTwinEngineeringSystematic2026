# Conclusions

Regarding the technological architectures and deployment environments (O1),
educational digital twins are realised predominantly as hybrid physical–virtual
and immersive configurations rather than as fully instrumented, closed-loop
systems. The field's technical ambition is rising—bidirectional and occasionally
AI-augmented twins are increasingly described—but the terminology frequently
outpaces verified synchronisation, and the convergence with artificial
intelligence remains incipient and largely unvalidated. The technological maturity
of the field is therefore, at present, more often asserted than demonstrated, and
claims of completeness are best read as descriptive rather than evidential until
synchronisation and fidelity are actually measured.

On the pedagogical and curricular dimension (O2), digital twins are consistently
embedded in active, experiential and project-based designs and span a widening
range of engineering disciplines, signalling genuine pedagogical intent and
disciplinary breadth. Yet the instructional model is often left implicit, and the
competencies attributed to Industry 4.0/5.0 are seldom anchored to recognised
standards or validated with industry. The pedagogical case is thus plausible but
under-specified: technical capability and intentional instructional design are
advancing on parallel rather than integrated tracks.

With respect to the empirical evidence of learning outcomes (O3), the corpus
establishes feasibility and positive engagement, and a minority of methodologically
stronger studies report meaningful gains; these, however, sit within a
heterogeneous and weakly reported base in which inferential statistics and effect
sizes are the exception rather than the norm. The present evidence justifies
cautious optimism about engagement and proof of concept, but does not yet support
robust or generalisable claims of learning efficacy, particularly concerning skill
transfer and longer-term retention.

Concerning institutional benefits and barriers (O4), accessibility, educational
continuity and sustainability are argued far more often than they are quantified,
while the technical, pedagogical and institutional barriers are reported
unevenly. The presence of a substantial barriers-aware literature indicates that
the field is not uniformly promotional; even so, the recurring assertion of
benefits without a matched account of limitations counsels restraint. The
institutional value proposition remains largely rhetorical pending the
instrumentation of cost, environmental and equity claims.

Taken together (general objective), this review characterises a field that
reliably demonstrates feasibility, engagement and disciplinary breadth, yet whose
defining feature is a structural decoupling between the maturity of the twin and
the rigour of its evaluation, with competence and institutional claims outpacing
demonstrated outcomes. The contribution of this synthesis is not to adjudicate
whether digital twins "work", but to weight the available evidence and make this
decoupling—and the near-empty frontier where technical sophistication meets strong
evaluation—explicit. Digital twins in engineering education are best understood as
a promising but not yet evidentially consolidated approach, whose trajectory will
depend less on further technical demonstration than on the quality of the evidence
brought to bear on it. In this, the review offers engineering education research a
sharper criterion for judging progress—evidential quality rather than technical
novelty—and offers practice a caution against equating sophistication with
demonstrated learning.

Several recommendations follow. For researchers, the priority is evidential
consolidation: pre-registered, multi-institution studies on higher-maturity twins,
with explicit comparators, reported effect sizes, and the measurement of transfer
and retention rather than immediate satisfaction; AI and synchronisation components
should be validated against declared performance criteria. For educators, digital
twins should be paired with an explicit pedagogical model—grounded in
constructivist learning theory, constructive alignment, or learner-agency
frameworks—and aligned assessment, and competencies linked to recognised
standards. For institutions and tool developers, cost, sustainability,
accessibility, interoperability and synchronisation should be reported with metrics
rather than asserted. To support cumulative, verifiable progress, the full protocol,
screening trace, extraction dataset and analysis code underlying this review are
released openly (GitHub and Zenodo), so that the synthesis can be audited,
reused and extended. Finally, the review itself should be strengthened before any
update by registering the protocol, reporting the remaining inter-rater
reliability, and recovering the full texts that could not be retrieved, so that the
corpus on which these conclusions rest is as complete as the field allows.
