**Digital Twins in Engineering and STEM Higher Education: A Systematic Review of Architectures, Pedagogy, and the Quality of Learning Evidence**

# Abstract

**Background:** Practical laboratory training is central to engineering education yet constrained by cost, safety, access, and post-pandemic continuity. Digital twins (DTs) — virtual replicas bidirectionally coupled to physical assets — are increasingly proposed to address these limits, but the educational evidence is dispersed and uneven.

**Purpose:** This review characterises how DTs are designed, implemented, and evaluated in higher-education engineering and STEM settings, across four objectives: technological architecture and deployment environments (O1); pedagogy and Industry 4.0/5.0 competencies (O2); empirical learning outcomes (O3); and institutional benefits and barriers (O4).

**Scope/Method:** Following PRISMA 2020, five databases were searched. A terminological screen, three-agent semantic screening (with a moderator agent; inter-agent Fleiss' κ = 0.60), and human review yielded 126 eligible records, of which 65 retrievable studies (2018–2026) formed the corpus. Extraction was theme-conditional with double verification; the methodological quality of empirical studies was scored on a 0–6 composite and sensitivity-tested. Synthesis combined descriptive profiling, aggregation by objective, and an exploratory relational analysis (attribute-association network, multiple correspondence analysis, and a capability-by-evidence gap map).

**Results:** The corpus demonstrates feasibility and engagement and is disciplinarily broad, with a recent shift toward bidirectional, occasionally AI-augmented twins. However, the technical maturity of the twin is decoupled from the rigour of its evaluation: architectures are described more than instrumented, pedagogy is active but seldom named, and the learning evidence is weak-dominated, with only a minority of rigorous studies reporting substantial effects.

**Conclusions:** Educational DTs are promising but not yet evidentially consolidated. We outline priorities for pre-registered, transfer-oriented, and well-reported research.
